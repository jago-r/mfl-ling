How to pronounce <ñ> in Spanish

Spanish makes a difference between <n> and <ñ>. The former is pronounced the same as in English. The letter <ñ> sounds similar to the first ‘n’ in ‘onion’ in English, but not exactly the same.

When you make an /n/ sound in English, notice the position of your tongue. It should be at the front of your mouth, right behind the teeth. When you say a word like ‘yes’, for the first sound, the tongue is further back, on the roof of your mouth.

In English, words like ‘onion’ have this n sound with the tongue at the front of your mouth followed immediately by the y sound with your tongue further back. In the International Phonetic Alphabet, this is represented as the sequence /nj/.

In Spanish <ñ> is a single sound, not a sequence of two. To produce it, keep your tongue in the position it is in for y, then make an ‘n’ sound without moving your tongue forward. In the International Phonetic Alphabet, this is represented as /ɲ/, or ‘n’ with a tail on its left side.

1. Practice saying the following pairs of Spanish words, paying attention to the position of your tongue. You don’t want to mix the first pair up!

  • Año (year) – ano (anus)
  • Uña (fingernail) – una (one)

2. Practice saying the following common Spanish words:

  • Niña (girl)
  • Señor (mister)
  • España (Spain)
  • Mañana (morning)
